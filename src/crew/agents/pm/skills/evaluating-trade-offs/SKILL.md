# Evaluating Trade-offs

Help the user make clearer decisions between competing options using frameworks and mental models from 40 product leaders.

## How to Help

When the user asks for help evaluating trade-offs:

1. **Understand the decision context** - Ask what they're optimizing for and what makes this decision difficult
2. **Identify the real constraints** - Help distinguish between actual constraints and assumed ones
3. **Surface hidden costs** - Quantify maintenance burden, opportunity cost, and second-order effects
4. **Apply the right framework** - Use weighted criteria matrices or simple continuation tests like "would I start this today?"

## Core Principles

### Optimize for order-of-magnitude, not precision

Focus on whether one option is dramatically better, not marginally better.

### Apply the would-I-start-this-today test

Ignore sunk costs entirely. Ask whether you would begin this effort with today's knowledge.

### Think more, ship better

Do not default to "let's just try it." Rigorous upfront thinking eliminates weak ideas before they consume engineering resources.

### Accept worse first for long-term gains

Meaningful change often requires accepting short-term decline to get to the better long-term outcome.

### Create decision tenets to eliminate recurring debates

Identify debates your team has repeatedly and create a tenet that decides the direction once.

### Quantify countervailing metrics

Assign dollar values to negative user actions to make objective trade-offs against short-term gains.

### Use a weighted criteria matrix

Create options as rows and weighted criteria as columns. The process often reveals the answer before the math is even finished.

### Present clear either/or choices to leadership

Communicate what the team is not doing as clearly as what it is doing.

### Separate can from should

Do not let feasibility constraints dominate strategic thinking.

### Diagnose with data, treat with design

Use data to identify problems and gaps, but rely on design and intuition to invent solutions.

### Beware the cost of analysis itself

Evaluate whether the person-hours spent analyzing a decision exceed the upside of the improvement.

### Identify who loses

When launching a feature, explicitly identify who will lose and whether the trade-off is acceptable.

## Questions to Help Users

- "What are you optimizing for: today, this quarter, or this year?"
- "If you weren't already committed to this, would you start it today?"
- "What's the full all-in cost of each option?"
- "Is this decision reversible or a one-way door?"
- "Who loses if you choose option A?"
- "What would your 5-year future self wish you had done?"

## Common Mistakes to Flag

- **False precision**
- **Sunk cost fallacy**
- **Analysis paralysis**
- **Ignoring second-order effects**
- **Defaulting to your skillset**
